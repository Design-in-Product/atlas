# Series 9 Scope: 1453–2026 CE

## Overview

Extend the Dynamic Atlas from its current endpoint (1453 CE, Fall of Constantinople) to the present day. This is the most data-dense period in human history — the rate of political change accelerates from roughly one major shift per century to dozens per decade by the 20th century.

## Political Entities — Tiered System

### Tier 1: Great Powers / Empires / Blocs
Always visible at any zoom. These define the era's geopolitical structure.

**Early modern (1453–1763):**
- Ottoman Empire, Spanish Empire (→ Habsburg), Portuguese Empire, French Kingdom/Empire, England/Britain, Holy Roman Empire, Polish-Lithuanian Commonwealth, Russian Tsardom/Empire, Safavid Persia → Afsharid → Qajar, Mughal Empire, Ming → Qing China, Tokugawa Japan

**Imperial peak (1763–1914):**
- British Empire, French Empire, Russian Empire, Austro-Hungarian Empire, Ottoman Empire, German Empire (post-1871), Qing China, Japanese Empire (post-1868), US (post-1783), Spanish Empire (shrinking)

**20th century (1914–1991):**
- Pre-WWI: same as above
- Interwar: British Empire, French Empire, USSR, US, Japanese Empire, Nationalist China, Nazi Germany, Fascist Italy
- Cold War: NATO bloc, Warsaw Pact bloc, Non-Aligned Movement, PRC

**Post–Cold War (1991–2024):**
- US-led (NATO/allies), China + partners, Russia, EU (as a quasi-entity?), or just show nation-states — tier 1 may not apply the same way

### Tier 2: States / Colonies / Dominions
Visible at medium zoom. This is the main working layer for most of the period.

**Color family system:**
Each Tier 1 power gets a color family. Tier 2 entities use shades/tints of their parent's color.

### Tier 3: (Deferred)
Fine-grained — HRE principalities, Indian princely states, pre-colonial African kingdoms under colonial overlay. Architecture supports it via `tier: 3` property, but data authoring deferred.

### Rendering Logic
```
function shouldRenderFeature(feat, zoomLevel) {
  if (feat.tier === 1) return true;                    // always
  if (feat.tier === 2 && zoomLevel >= 4) return true;  // medium zoom
  if (feat.tier === 3 && zoomLevel >= 6) return true;  // high zoom
  return false;
}
```

At zoom 2–3 (global view), you see empires and blocs. Zoom to 4–5, you see colonies and states. Zoom to 6+, you see principalities and substates.

### Alliance/Bloc Overlay (New Layer Type)
Alliances rendered as a separate toggle layer (like religion/language), not as political entities. Dashed borders, very low opacity, distinct from political fills.

Key alliances to show:
- Triple Alliance / Triple Entente (pre-WWI)
- Axis / Allies (WWII)
- NATO / Warsaw Pact (Cold War)
- EU (post-Maastricht)
- Commonwealth of Nations (post-British Empire, as a dotted overlay)

## Language Layer (1453–2026)

### Approach: Vernacular Ground Truth
Show what people actually spoke, not the imperial administrative language. The colonial language doesn't replace the substrate — it layers on top.

### Key Dynamics to Track

**European consolidation (1453–1700):**
- National languages crystallize: standardized French, Castilian, Portuguese, English, German, Russian, Dutch
- Latin retreats from vernacular use (persists in church/academia)
- Celtic languages continue shrinking (Irish, Welsh, Scottish Gaelic, Breton)

**Colonial language spread (1500–1960):**
- Spanish across Latin America (overlaying Nahuatl, Quechua, Maya, Guaraní etc.)
- Portuguese in Brazil, Africa, Goa
- English in North America, India, Africa, Oceania
- French in West Africa, Indochina, Caribbean
- Dutch in Indonesia (limited)
- Arabic continues spreading in sub-Saharan Africa via trade/religion

**Approach for colonial languages:** Show the indigenous language as the base zone. When a colonial language becomes widely spoken (not just by administrators), it could be indicated by a hatching pattern or dual-color, but that's a rendering complexity. Simpler approach: for regions where the colonial language *replaced* the indigenous one (Americas, Australia), transition the zone's label. For regions where it became a second language (India, Africa), keep the indigenous language primary.

**20th century:**
- Hindi/Urdu partition (1947)
- Hebrew revival (Israel, 1948)
- Mandarin standardization in China
- Language death accelerates (small languages disappearing)
- English as global lingua franca (hard to show geographically — it's everywhere as L2)

### Implementation
Continue the existing `LANGUAGE_KEYFRAMES` array, adding 23 new keyframe entries matching the political years. For the 1453 entry we already have data — it's the bridge keyframe.

## Religion Layer (1453–2026)

### Approach: Schisms as the Primary Dynamic
The user specifically flagged schisms as the most interesting thing to track. This period has several major ones:

### Key Schisms & Splits

**Christianity:**
- **Protestant Reformation (1517+):** Catholic → Catholic + Lutheran + Reformed/Calvinist + Anglican
  - 1555: Augsburg settles Lutheran/Catholic boundary in HRE
  - 1600: Netherlands Calvinist, Scandinavia Lutheran, England Anglican, Scotland Presbyterian
  - Counter-Reformation: Spain, Italy, Poland, southern HRE remain Catholic
  - France: Catholic majority, Huguenot minority → revocation of Edict of Nantes (1685)
- **Further Protestant fragmentation:** Puritans, Methodists, Baptists — harder to show geographically in Europe but relevant in North America
- **Colonial Christianity:** Catholic (Spanish/Portuguese/French colonies) vs. Protestant (British/Dutch colonies)
- **Orthodox:** Largely stable geography (Russia, Balkans, Greece, Ethiopia) but Russian expansion carries it east

**Islam:**
- Sunni/Shia geography largely stable after 1500, but:
  - **Safavid conversion of Iran (1501+):** Iran becomes definitively Shia
  - Sunni: Ottoman lands, Mughal India, Southeast Asia, most of Africa
  - Shia: Iran, southern Iraq, parts of Lebanon/Yemen
  - Ibadi: Oman
- Colonial period: Islam continues spreading in sub-Saharan Africa and SE Asia (not via conquest but via trade)
- 20th century: Wahhabism in Saudi Arabia; political Islam movements

**Other:**
- Sikhism emerges (Punjab, 1500s+)
- Bahá'í Faith (Iran, 1844+)
- Decline of Tengrism/shamanism as Turkic/Mongol peoples consolidate under Islam/Buddhism
- 20th century secularism: hard to show geographically (every country has it to some degree)
- Hindu/Buddhist geography largely stable; Buddhism in Tibet under pressure post-1950

### Color Palette for Schisms
Need sub-colors within Christianity:
- Catholic: #7E57C2 (existing purple) or darker
- Lutheran: #AB47BC (lighter purple)
- Reformed/Calvinist: #CE93D8 (lavender)
- Anglican: #9575CD (blue-purple)
- Orthodox: #5E35B1 (deep violet) — already distinct
- Protestant (generic, for Americas/later): #BA68C8 (medium purple)

Islam:
- Sunni: #4CAF50 (existing green)
- Shia: #00C853 (bright green) or #81C784 (light green)
- Ibadi: #A5D6A7 (pale green)

This keeps the "Christianity = purple family, Islam = green family" convention while showing internal differentiation.

## Trade Routes Layer (1453–2026)

### Approach: Three Eras + The Mesh Fade

**Era 1 — Age of Sail (1453–1850):**
The most visually interesting period for trade routes. Named routes with clear paths.

New routes to add:
- **Cape Route** (1498+): Lisbon → West Africa → Cape of Good Hope → India. Portuguese, then Dutch/British.
- **Manila Galleon** (1565–1815): Acapulco → Manila. Spanish. The first Pacific trade route.
- **Atlantic Triangle** (1500s–1800s): Europe → West Africa → Americas → Europe. Including the slave trade (sensitive — note in desc).
- **Dutch East Indies Route** (1600s+): Amsterdam → Cape → Batavia (Jakarta).
- **British India Route** (1600s+): London → Cape / Suez → Bombay/Calcutta.
- **Fur Trade Routes** (1600s–1800s): Hudson Bay → interior North America. French and British.
- **Silver Fleet** (1500s–1700s): Americas (Potosí, Mexico) → Spain.
- **Tea/Opium Route** (1700s–1800s): Canton → London (tea); India → Canton (opium). British.

Existing routes that continue:
- Silk Road: declining post-1453 (Ottoman tolls, sea routes competing), mostly dead by 1600
- Trans-Saharan: continues through period, gradually declining
- Indian Ocean: absorbed into European-controlled shipping lanes
- Spice Route: now run by Portuguese/Dutch instead of Arab traders

**Era 2 — Industrial Networks (1850–1950):**
Transition from named routes to infrastructure.

- **Suez Canal** (1869+): Collapses the Cape Route for East-bound traffic.
- **Panama Canal** (1914+): Atlantic-Pacific shortcut.
- **Trans-Siberian Railway** (1904+): Moscow → Vladivostok. (Land route — new rendering type?)
- **Transatlantic Cable** (1866+): Telegraph, then telephone. (Submarine line.)
- **Steamship Lanes**: Regular scheduled service replacing wind-dependent routes. More routes, more standardized.

**Era 3 — The Global Mesh (1950–2026):**
This is where the creative visualization comes in. Ideas:

1. **Densifying network**: 1950s–1980s, show major containerized shipping lanes as a thickening web of routes. More and more lines appear.
2. **The mesh moment**: ~1990s, the network becomes so dense it's almost a solid web. Show this visually — perhaps the individual routes merge into a luminous mesh overlay.
3. **Fade to ubiquity**: 2000+, the mesh fades to a very subtle background glow (like "trade is everywhere, showing individual routes no longer makes sense"). Or: keep the mesh but at very low opacity, with a few key chokepoints highlighted (Suez, Panama, Strait of Malacca, Strait of Hormuz).
4. **Internet overlay?**: Could show submarine internet cables as a separate route type in the 2000s — they follow surprisingly similar paths to 19th century telegraph cables.

### Implementation
Extend `TRADE_ROUTE_KEYFRAMES` with new entries for each year. Status system already supports `emerging → active → peak → declining → gone`. For the mesh era, could introduce a new route type (`type: "mesh"`) rendered differently — perhaps as a semi-transparent web rather than individual polylines.

## Governance Layer (New — Phase 9.7)

### Approach: V-Dem Choropleth Overlay

A color-gradient overlay showing democratic vs. authoritarian governance, based on the Varieties of Democracy (V-Dem) dataset — the most comprehensive and nuanced available, covering 1789–present with hundreds of indicators.

**Why V-Dem over Polity or Freedom House:**
- Polity5 covers 1800–2018 but has known biases (rated the US 9/10 during slavery, 10/10 during Jim Crow; colonial territories are simply "no data" rather than registering as authoritarian). Polity was discontinued as of 2020.
- Freedom House covers only 1972–present and uses a coarse 3-category system (Free / Partly Free / Not Free).
- V-Dem covers 1789–present, uses multiple democracy dimensions (electoral, liberal, participatory, deliberative, egalitarian), and classifies into 4 regime types: closed autocracy, electoral autocracy, electoral democracy, liberal democracy. Its 2025 report found liberal democracy levels are back to 1985 levels globally.
- All indices have methodological critiques (V-Dem's expert-coded data has time-varying bias concerns). The atlas should label the source explicitly.

**Rendering:**
- Choropleth gradient over political entities: deep red (closed autocracy) → orange (electoral autocracy) → yellow (electoral democracy) → blue (liberal democracy)
- Toggle layer like religion/language, independent opacity slider
- Legend: "Governance: V-Dem Liberal Democracy Index" with explicit "Scores are one dataset's assessment — contested and approximate" disclaimer
- For the pre-1789 era (our existing 500 BCE–1453 keyframes): effectively uniform autocracy, with rare exceptions noted in tooltip (Athenian democracy, Roman Republic, Icelandic Althing, etc.). Could render as a subtle uniform wash or simply not activate before 1789.

**Data pipeline:**
- V-Dem publishes country-year datasets as CSV. We'd extract the `v2x_libdem` (liberal democracy) score for each country-year matching our keyframe years.
- Map V-Dem country codes to our political entities
- For colonial territories: inherit the colonizer's score? Or show as "no self-governance" (deep red)? The latter is more honest — people living under colonial rule did not have democratic governance regardless of the metropole's domestic politics.

**Future option:** Offer V-Dem vs. Polity as a toggle (two different lenses on the same question), once the infrastructure is in place. This makes the "which index you use matters" point experientially rather than didactically.

**Important framing notes:**
- This layer shows *institutional governance structures*, not whether a country is "good" or "bad"
- Democratic backsliding is visible as color shift — the 2010s regression shows up naturally
- Oligarchic capture (where formal democratic institutions exist but real power is concentrated) is partially captured by V-Dem's "electoral democracy vs. liberal democracy" distinction, though no index fully captures it
- The layer makes no claim about which system is better — it maps what exists

## Disputed Territories (Integrated into Political Layer)

### Approach: Certainty-First Cartography

Rather than making calls about who "owns" contested land, show certainty where it exists and ambiguity where it exists. This follows established cartographic standards (ICA conventions, the "Blurry Borders" atlas methodology).

**Rendering spec:**

| Zone Type | Fill | Border | Label |
|-----------|------|--------|-------|
| Undisputed territory | Solid fill, entity color | Solid line | Entity name |
| Disputed territory | Hatched fill, lighter tint of administering entity's color | Dash-dot line in primary claimant's color | "Disputed" (on map); "Administered by X, claimed by Y" (in popup) |

**Design principles:**
- Show the *de facto* administering power's color (lighter tint) — because that's the observable reality
- Show the primary claimant via border color — because that's the contested claim
- Don't attempt to show third+ claimants visually (too much info) — mention in popup text
- Use neutral language: "administered by" not "controlled by" or "belonging to"
- Each entity's undisputed area = total claimed minus any disputed zones. This is the "I know for sure this is part of this country" principle.

**Data source for modern era:**
Natural Earth provides a disputed areas shapefile derived from the CIA World Factbook boundary database. For historical eras, disputed zones are fewer and can be hand-authored (e.g., the Ottoman-Safavid borderlands, the Hundred Years' War territories).

**Key modern disputed areas to include (2024 keyframe):**
Kashmir, Crimea, Golan Heights, West Bank/Gaza, Western Sahara, Aksai Chin/Arunachal Pradesh, South China Sea islands, Cyprus (north), Taiwan (status), Kuril Islands, Dokdo/Takeshima — plus any that emerge between design and implementation.

**Feature schema addition:**
```
{
  name: "Kashmir",
  type: "disputed",           // new type
  administeredBy: "India",    // de facto
  claimedBy: "Pakistan",      // primary claimant
  otherClaimants: ["China"],  // additional (shown in popup only)
  color: "#C62828",           // lighter tint of administering entity
  borderColor: "#2E7D32",     // claimant color
  desc: "Administered by India (Jammu & Kashmir), claimed by Pakistan and partially by China (Aksai Chin)."
}
```

## Connectivity Layer (New — Phase 9.8, Modern Era Only)

### Approach: From Routes to Flows to Ubiquity

For the late modern era (~1960–2024), an optional layer that shows the world as a network of connections rather than a patchwork of territories. This is the "post-geographic" view — not replacing the political layer but offering an alternative lens.

**Why this matters:**
Parag Khanna's *Connectography* thesis: connectivity (supply chains, infrastructure, data flows) increasingly matters more than sovereignty lines. The real power nodes are cities, ports, and corridors rather than nation-states. This layer lets users *see* that shift by toggling between the political view and the connectivity view.

**Rendering phases:**

| Era | Visualization | Description |
|-----|--------------|-------------|
| 1960–1980 | Thickening routes | Major containerized shipping lanes, air routes as polylines. More appear each keyframe. |
| 1980–1995 | Dense mesh + cables | Shipping mesh thickens. Submarine telegraph/phone/internet cables appear (they follow strikingly similar paths to 19th century telegraph routes). Internet backbone lines emerge. |
| 1995 transition | Mesh peaks | The network is so dense it's nearly a solid luminous web across all oceans and continents. This is the visual "globalization moment." |
| 2000–2024 | Mesh fades, chokepoints glow | The mesh fades to a subtle background wash (trade is ubiquitous, showing individual routes is no longer informative). Key chokepoints remain highlighted: Suez, Panama, Strait of Malacca, Strait of Hormuz, Bab el-Mandeb. These are the bottlenecks where geography still constrains the post-geographic world. |

**Implementation:**
- New route type: `type: "mesh"` — rendered as a web of thin, semi-transparent lines rather than individual named polylines
- Chokepoints: `type: "chokepoint"` — rendered as pulsing dots or highlighted short segments
- Cables: `type: "cable"` — submarine lines, thin, distinct color (maybe electric blue)
- The layer is only active for keyframes >= 1960. Before that, the regular trade routes layer handles everything.

**What this layer does NOT show:**
- Corporate influence or market dominance (too politically loaded, geographically ambiguous)
- Internet traffic flows (interesting but ephemeral and hard to source reliably)
- Specific trade volumes (would need a different data approach — maybe later as a "trade intensity" heat map)

**War-related alliances in earlier eras (1453–1914):**

- Holy League(s) — various anti-Ottoman coalitions
- Habsburg-Bourbon rivalry axis
- Concert of Europe (1815–1914) — the Congress system
- The "Great Game" alignment (British India vs. Russian expansion in Central Asia)

**Rendering:** Same as religion/language layer pattern — independent toggle, opacity slider, dashed borders. Alliance fill is a single color per alliance at very low opacity (0.1), with the alliance name as a label.

**Modern blocs note:** BRICS+ (Brazil, Russia, India, China, South Africa + Saudi Arabia, Egypt, Ethiopia, UAE, Iran as of 2024) shows up in the 2024 keyframe. The atlas shows institutional membership facts, not predictions about which bloc is "winning." The viewer can draw their own conclusions from what they see.

## Resolved Design Decisions

1. **Disputed territories**: Certainty-first cartography. Solid fill for undisputed, hatched lighter tint for disputed (administering entity's color), dash-dot border in claimant's color. Neutral language. Third+ claimants in popup only.

2. **Colonial borders vs. pre-colonial entities**: Show all three phases — pre-colonial entities in their own colors, colonial territories in imperial color families, post-colonial states in their own new colors. The color shift from "shade of British red" to "own distinct color" visually narrates decolonization.

3. **Trade mesh threshold**: ~1960 to ~1990 densification, ~1995 transition year, post-2000 fade to ubiquity with chokepoints. Internet cables appear in the 2000s keyframes as a distinct visual element.

4. **Alliance criteria**: Institutional alliances (5+ years, mutual commitments) for the modern era. War-defining alliances for earlier eras. Facts only, no editorial about which bloc is ascendant.

5. **Tier 1 in the modern era**: Post-1991, Tier 1 becomes "major power" highlighting rather than "empire containing sub-entities." The US, China, Russia, India, EU rendered as Tier 1 nation-states (always visible, maybe slightly thicker borders or brighter fill) rather than as blocs containing other countries. The alliance layer handles bloc visualization separately.

6. **Governance mapping**: V-Dem dataset, choropleth gradient, explicit source labeling. Colonial territories rendered as deep red (no self-governance) regardless of metropole's domestic politics. Pre-1789 is uniform autocracy with tooltip exceptions. No synthesis across indices — show one lens at a time.

7. **Corporate influence**: Out of scope for this phase. The connectivity layer gestures at post-national power structures without naming corporate actors.

## Open Questions (Remaining)

1. **V-Dem data availability for our exact keyframe years.** V-Dem is country-year, so we'd interpolate or snap to nearest year. Need to verify coverage for all 23 proposed years.

2. **How to render the governance layer for non-sovereign entities** (colonies, occupied territories, mandates). The "deep red" approach is a simplification — some colonies had limited self-governance (British Dominions had parliaments). Maybe a gradient within the "not independent" category?

3. **Connectivity layer data sourcing.** Submarine cable routes are well-documented (TeleGeography). Shipping lanes are available from AIS data. Air routes are available but voluminous. How much fidelity do we need for a stylized visualization?

4. **Pre-colonial entity persistence during colonial era.** Some pre-colonial entities continued to exist under colonial rule (Indian princely states, African kingdoms with treaties). Do they show at Tier 3 under the colonial overlay, or only in the pre-colonial and post-colonial phases?
